Launching a crypto derivatives exchange used to mean a multi-year engineering project. With a white-label engine, the timeline collapses to weeks — but the *sequence* still matters. Here's the step-by-step path.

## Step 0: Strategy before software

Before anything technical, answer three questions:

- **Who is this for?** A region, a community, a trader segment. "Everyone" is not a wedge — see [go-to-market](/blog/go-to-market-new-crypto-exchange).
- **Which products?** Perpetuals first (they dominate [volume](/blog/how-crypto-exchanges-make-money)), then options or dated futures.
- **Which jurisdiction?** This decision shapes everything downstream — see [Global Licensing Guide](/blog/crypto-derivatives-licensing-global-guide).

## Step 1: Licensing & compliance

Secure the right authorization for your jurisdiction and product. Remember that **derivatives are regulated more strictly than spot** (MiFID II in the EU, CFTC in the US). In parallel, design your [KYC/AML program](/blog/kyc-aml-for-crypto-exchanges) — it's a licence condition, not an afterthought. This is usually the longest pole; start it early.

## Step 2: Choose your infrastructure path

Build or buy? For most operators, white-label wins on time, cost, and risk — the decision framework is in [White-Label vs. Building](/blog/white-label-vs-building-crypto-exchange). Then [choose a provider](/blog/how-to-choose-white-label-exchange-provider) against a clear checklist.

## Step 3: Technical integration

Connect your front end to the provider's APIs and stand up the backend: trading engine, order books, wallets, and market data. With a white-label engine, this is configuration and integration work — not building a matching engine from scratch.

## Step 4: Platform customization

Configure what's yours: branding and UI, the trading pairs and leverage tiers you'll offer, your [fee schedule](/blog/maker-taker-fees-explained), and risk parameters. This is where your venue becomes *yours*.

## Step 5: Liquidity

Line up [liquidity](/blog/crypto-exchange-liquidity-cold-start) so your order books are deep on day one. Thin books are the fastest way to lose your first users. A white-label engine typically aggregates this for you.

## Step 6: Testing & training

Validate edge cases end-to-end — order types, [liquidations](/blog/how-liquidation-price-is-calculated), funding, deposits/withdrawals — and train your team. Then a soft launch with limited users before full go-live.

## Step 7: Go-live & support

Launch with SLA-backed support and monitoring in place, then shift focus to growth: acquisition, incentives, and retention.

<Callout title="Realistic timeline">
On proven white-label infrastructure, steps 2–7 typically run **4–8 weeks**. Licensing (Step 1) runs in parallel and is usually the gating item — plan it first and give it the most lead time.
</Callout>

## The takeaway

Launching is now a sequencing problem, not an engineering marathon: nail strategy and licensing early, pick the right infrastructure path, then integrate, customize, and go live in weeks. The hard, slow parts are compliance and growth — which is exactly why offloading the engine makes sense.
