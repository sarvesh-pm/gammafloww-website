Leverage is the headline feature of crypto derivatives — "up to 125x" is a common banner. But the number is widely misunderstood. Leverage doesn't just multiply your gains; it shrinks the room you have to be wrong.

## Notional vs. margin

Leverage is simply the ratio between the position you control (**notional**) and the collateral you post (**margin**):

`Notional = Margin × Leverage`

Post $1,000 at 25x, and you control $25,000 of exposure. A 1% move on $25,000 is $250 — a 25% swing on your $1,000 margin. That asymmetry is the whole point, and the whole danger.

## Higher leverage = closer liquidation

Here's the part the "125x" banner doesn't advertise: the more leverage you use, the smaller the price move needed to liquidate you. As a rough rule, the adverse move to liquidation is about **1 / leverage**:

| Leverage | Approx. move to liquidation |
|---|---|
| 5x | ~20% |
| 10x | ~10% |
| 25x | ~4% |
| 50x | ~2% |
| 125x | ~0.8% |

At 125x, a **sub-1% move** against you ends the position. (The maintenance margin rate makes it slightly tighter still.) See the exact mechanics in [How Liquidation Price Is Calculated](/blog/how-liquidation-price-is-calculated).

## Why exchanges offer it anyway

High leverage attracts volume — and volume is [how exchanges make money](/blog/how-crypto-exchanges-make-money). But it also concentrates risk, which is why a venue needs robust margin tiers, maintenance-margin requirements, and a fast liquidation engine. Responsible venues pair high maximum leverage with tiered limits that reduce the max as position size grows ([Bybit: liquidation & margin](https://www.bybit.com/en/help-center/article/Liquidation-Price-Calculation-under-Isolated-Mode-Unified-Trading-Account)).

<Callout title="Takeaway">
Leverage multiplies exposure by shrinking your margin buffer. 125x sounds powerful, but it means liquidation on a &lt;1% move. For an operator, offering high leverage safely is a risk-engineering problem, not a marketing one.
</Callout>
